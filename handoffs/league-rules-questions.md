# Questions for NYC coed leagues about roster/gender rules

Drafted July 19 2026, reworked July 21 2026. Raw material for emails — edit
freely. **Everything below is framed at 7v7** so the leagues line up against
each other.

League rules websites:

- NYC Footy: https://www.nycfooty.com/league-rules
- NYC Footy 50/50 & FLIP: https://www.nycfooty.com/50-50-flip-female-majority-leagues
- NYC Soccer Collective: https://nycsoccercollective.net/league-rules-7v7
- NY Coed Soccer: https://nycoedsoccer.com/rules/
- Urban Soccer: https://www.urbansoccer.com/rules
- Volo Sports: https://www.volosports.com/l/4bd71be7-5047-4f95-a19d-0380b33361b4 (rules under "Gameplay & Rosters" and "Basic Rules 7x7")

What the app actually needs from each league is three numbers plus one
behavior: **full side** (7), **minimum to take the field**, the **binding
gender constraint** (a women floor, a men ceiling, or a ratio), and **what
happens when a team is short on women** (play down, and how far — or forfeit).

We've filled in most of that from each league's published rules. The
questions below are only the genuine gaps — we're not re-asking anything the
rules already answer, to keep these short for the officials.

A note on goalkeepers: the app models the keeper as an **explicit concept**
and speaks each league's own vocabulary ("2 women outfield, keeper doesn't
count"; "4 outfield men, a 5th may keep") — it never silently folds the keeper
into an adjusted number, because doing so both misjudges the woman-in-goal
edge case and stops the app from explaining or advising in terms the captain
recognizes. So we need each league's keeper scoping spelled out. Two leagues
already publish theirs (Soccer Collective; NY Coed), so we don't re-ask; the
other three leave it unstated, so we do. (Keeper scoping only changes whether
a *legal lineup is possible* in the men-ceiling and ratio leagues; for a plain
women floor it rarely does — but the app still needs it to display the rule
and advise correctly, so we ask wherever it isn't published.)

## NYC Footy — standard coed (7v7)

**What we read:** full side 7, minimum 5 to start and finish, 2 women
required; short on women → play down one player per missing woman (1 woman →
6, 0 women → 5); no stated maximum on men.

**What the website says** *(nycfooty.com/league-rules)*:
- Minimum to field: "In a 7v7 or 8v8 league, a minimum of five (5) players is
  required to start and finish a match."
- Female minimum: "In our 5v5 leagues there is a minimum of one (1) female; in
  our 6v6, 7v7 and 8v8 leagues, a minimum of two (2) females; in 9v9 and 11v11
  leagues, a minimum of three (3) women is required at all times."
- Play-down: "If a team does not have the required amount of female players,
  they must play down a player. For instance, in a 7v7 league, teams playing
  with one (1) female will play with six (6) total players and teams playing
  with no (0) females will play with five (5) total players."
- 3-goal option: "During the regular season only, in leagues with 2+ minimum
  female requirements, a team without the required amount of female players has
  a choice to give the opponent 3 goals and replace one female spot with a
  non-male player."

**Question:** Does the keeper count toward the 2-women requirement, or must
both women be outfield players? And may the keeper be any gender? (Everything
else is already spelled out on the page — play down one per missing woman,
zero women → a legal 5-a-side, no forfeit — so there's nothing there to ask.)

## NYC Footy — 50/50 and FLIP (7v7)

**What we read:** 50/50 = "a minimum of 50% of field players must be female";
FLIP = "the majority of players on the pitch must be female." Your main rules
page lists, for 7v7, 3 women for 50/50 and 5 women for FLIP.

**What the website says**:
- Ratios *(nycfooty.com/50-50-flip-female-majority-leagues)*: "A minimum of
  50% of field players must be female." (50/50) and "The majority of players
  on the pitch must be female." (FLIP)
- 7v7 counts *(nycfooty.com/league-rules)*: "Our standard 7v7 FLIP leagues
  require a minimum of 5 female players on the pitch. Our FLIP 50/50 leagues
  require a minimum of 3 female players on the pitch in our standard 7v7
  format." (Note the "5 on the pitch" for FLIP is what sits awkwardly against
  "the majority" — a majority of 7 is 4 — which is why we ask.)

**Questions:**
1. In 7v7, does 50/50's "field players" exclude the keeper — so 3 of the 6
   outfield players must be women, keeper any gender — while FLIP's "players
   on the pitch" includes the keeper? And is the FLIP number really 5 of 7,
   or is it a simple majority (4 of 7)? The published wording and the "5"
   don't obviously agree.
2. If a team can't meet the ratio, do they play down like standard coed, or
   forfeit?

## NYC Soccer Collective (7v7)

**What we read:** 7 a side, minimum 4 roster players at kickoff+5 for an
official game; maximum 4 men as outfield field players, keeper excluded and
either gender — so at most 5 men total; keeper counts toward neither the min
nor the max; "generally you'll have 2 women" but no stated women minimum;
short on women → play down, male max holds "under any circumstances."

**What the website says** *(nycsoccercollective.net/league-rules-7v7)*:
- Minimum to field: "TEAMS must have minimum 4 ROSTER PLAYERS at 5 minutes
  past scheduled kickoff time for game to be official."
- Male maximum: "7v7 Teams may have a MAXIMUM of 4 men on the field as field
  players. (does not include goalkeeper)"
- Keeper: "Keeper may be male or female and does not count toward MAXIMUM or
  MINIMUM gender restrictions."
- Women (descriptive): "Generally you'll have 2 women on the field however
  there is no maximum of women on the field."
- Play-down: "You may also play with less than 2 women but you may not have
  more than the maximum men on the field as field players under any
  circumstances."

**No open questions.** The page already answers what we'd otherwise ask:
"You may also play with less than 2 women but you may not have more than the
maximum men on the field as field players under any circumstances" confirms 2
women is not a floor and the max-men rule is the only binding gender
constraint; the keeper line ("may be male or female and does not count toward
MAXIMUM or MINIMUM gender restrictions") and the 4-roster-player minimum
settle the rest. An all-male side — down to the 4-player minimum, with a male
keeper — is legal by these rules, never a forfeit purely for lacking women.

## NY Coed Soccer (nycoedsoccer.com) — most gaps; Richard's old league

**What we read (7-a-side):** full side 7; "two female players must be on the
outfield at all times," keeper excluded from that count; no stated maximum on
men; forfeit at 4 or fewer registered players. The published shortage rules
cover missing *players* but never a missing-*women* case.

**What the website says** *(nycoedsoccer.com/rules)*:
- Female requirement: "Two females players must be on the outfield at all
  times - the goalkeeper does not count towards this number."
- Pier 40: "Pier 40 games, however, require 3 females on the outfield."
- Forfeit threshold: "If a team has 4 or less registered players, then
  unfortunately the game will be forfeited and the result recorded as a 3-0
  loss." (Note: nothing here addresses a team that has enough players but too
  few women — the gap we're asking about.)

**Questions:**
1. The published shortage rules set a forfeit only at 4 or fewer registered
   players and never address a team that has enough players but too few women.
   So if a team arrives with fewer than 2 women on the outfield but more than
   4 players, what happens — play shorthanded, or forfeit? If shorthanded, is
   it one fewer total player per missing woman (as some leagues do)?
   Concretely: is a 5-player, zero-women side — above the 4-player forfeit
   line but short on women — playable, or a forfeit?
2. Your rules already say the keeper doesn't count toward the two women; just
   confirming the keeper may be any gender.

*(Pier 40 games require 3 women rather than 2 — same questions at three.)*

## Urban Soccer (7v7)

**What we read:** full side 7, minimum 5 to start; "minimum 2 women outfield
players or play shorthanded"; no stated maximum on men and no keeper gender
rule.

**What the website says** *(urbansoccer.com/rules)*:
- Gender requirement: "Coed 7v7 (minimum 2 women outfield players or play
  shorthanded)."
- Minimum to field: "Seven-A-Side: Minimum requirement 5 players for an
  official game."

**Questions:**
1. "Or play shorthanded" — by exactly how much? One fewer total player per
   missing woman (1 woman → 6, 0 women → 5)? And with zero women, is a 5-man
   side a legal official game, or is there a floor we're missing?
2. "Outfield" suggests the keeper doesn't count toward the 2 women — is that
   right, and may the keeper be any gender?

**Resolved (Urban reply, see Emails sent):** unit play-down — 1 woman → 6 (5
men + 1 woman), 0 women → 5 (all men), floor at 5. Keeper excluded from the
2-woman count and may be any gender. So Urban is engine type 1 (play-down),
same shape as NYC Footy standard, reducible to a men ceiling of 5. Fully
pinned; no open items.

## Volo Sports (7v7)

**What we read:** 7 players on the field, **maximum 5 men on the field at
once**, minimum 5 players to play, and a **hard minimum of 1 woman/non-binary
player** — so the smallest legal side is 4 men + 1 woman/non-binary. The
1-woman/NB requirement is built into the "minimum to play," so an all-male
group simply can't start (a forfeit, not a play-down). Volo is also the first
league to say "women/non-binary" rather than "women"/"females," and —
unusually — it puts **no special exception on the goalkeeper**: the limits
cover all seven players on the field. (Separately there's a season **roster**
rule — 12+ registered players including 4+ women/non-binary — but that's a
registration requirement, not a game-day one, so it's out of scope for
attendance.)

**Why this one is different:** every other league has a *single* binding
gender constraint — a men ceiling *or* a women floor, which (with unit
play-down) are the same rule in different words. Volo has **both, and they're
independent**: at a full side of 7 the max-5-men rule binds (it forces 2
non-men), but at 5–6 players the 1-woman/NB floor binds instead (4 men + 1
woman is a legal five). Neither implies the other, so the app genuinely needs
both knobs — a men ceiling *and* a women floor as separate parameters. This is
the **general two-knob form** the earlier leagues were one-knob special cases
of; Volo is the first to turn both on. And unlike the play-down leagues, its
women floor is **hard**: an all-male team forfeits rather than playing down. So
Volo is our first *confirmed* men-ceiling-plus-hard-women-floor league — the
exact shape NY Coed might also turn out to be.

A data-model note: "women/non-binary" — plus NYC Footy's and NSC's "non-male"
phrasings — confirms the requirement is really about **non-men**, and that
player gender isn't binary in the app's model. The app needs a category that
counts toward the requirement without being "woman."

**What the website says** *(volosports.com — "Gameplay & Rosters" and "Basic
Rules 7v7")*:
- On-field limit: "7v7 Coed (max. 5 men on the field at once)" and "7 players
  on field (5 men max. on the field)"
- Minimum to play: "Teams will need a minimum of 5 players to start with at
  least one woman/non-binary player" and "Minimum to play: 5 (including 1
  woman)"
- Roster (season): "A full team consists of 12+ players (with at least 4
  women/non-binary players)" and "Roster minimum: 12"

**Questions:**
1. The two pages word the minimum a little differently — "Minimum to play: 5
   (including 1 woman)" vs. "a minimum of 5 players to start with at least one
   woman/non-binary player." Does a **non-binary** player on their own satisfy
   it — i.e. do women and non-binary players count together toward the
   requirement? (We're assuming yes.)
2. To confirm the keeper isn't special: do the "5 men max" and "1
   woman/non-binary" limits count all seven on the field, keeper included — so
   a male keeper is one of the five men, and a woman/NB keeper satisfies the
   minimum? (No keeper exception, unlike some leagues.)

## Conclusions so far — draft engine design

Running notes, to be folded into DESIGN.md once the leagues reply. Nothing here
is in DESIGN.md yet.

### The canonical rules engine

For attendance the app answers one question: given who's coming (how many men,
and how many women/non-binary), can they field a legal side, and how big? Every
league reduces to a small parameter set applied to the players **on the field**:

- `fullSide` — players per side at full strength (each league runs several
  sizes; this doc uses 7v7 throughout)
- `minToPlay` — fewest players for a legal/official game (else forfeit)
- `menCeiling` — max men on the field (optional)
- `womenFloor` — min women/non-binary on the field (optional)
- `floorType` — when the floor isn't met: `play-down` (soft) or `forfeit` (hard)
- `keeperScoping` — keeper counted in the on-field totals, or carved out

The men-ceiling and women-floor are **independent knobs**. A soft women-floor
with unit play-down is mathematically identical to a men-ceiling of
`fullSide − floor` (the NYC Footy ⇄ NSC equivalence), so a league with only one
of them can be stored either way. We keep both as explicit parameters anyway,
because (a) some leagues set both at once (Volo), and (b) the UI must speak each
league's own words, never a flattened number.

### Engine types observed

1. **Play-down (soft)** — shortfall shrinks the legal side; reducible to a men
   ceiling. NYC Footy standard, NSC, Urban.
2. **Hard floor (forfeit)** — a women/NB minimum baked into `minToPlay`;
   all-male = forfeit. Volo (confirmed); NY Coed (pending — could be this or #1).
3. **Ratio** — required women scale with players on the field. NYC Footy
   FLIP/50-50. Deferred behind a "contact us" disclaimer for the MVP.

### Per-league status

**All numbers here are for 7v7**, used only as a common yardstick for comparing
leagues in this document. Each league applies the *same* rule shape across
several side sizes (5v5, 6v6, 8v8, 9v9, 11v11, …); only the numbers change — the
women/NB floor, the men ceiling, and the minimum-to-play all scale with the
format. The engine parameters are per-format; 7v7 is just the row we've filled
in.

| League | minToPlay | men max | women/NB min | short on women | keeper | still open |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| NYC Footy std | 5 | (5, via play-down) | 2 | play-down 1/missing | ? | keeper scoping |
| NYC Footy FLIP/50-50 | ? | — | ratio (5? / 3 of 7) | ? | ? | deferred; all asked |
| NSC | 4 | 5 (4 outfield + male GK) | none | play-down | excluded, any gender | none |
| NY Coed | 5 (forfeit ≤4) | none | 2 (Pier 40: 3) | **PENDING** | excluded | floorType + GK gender |

### Cross-cutting decisions

- **Keeper is an explicit concept, never flattened into a number.** Store
  `keeperScoping` per league and drive both the legality check and the advice
  text from it. Flattening misjudges the woman-in-goal edge case and breaks
  league-native display.
- **Two categories, always — and only ever two.** Every one of these systems
  has a single dominant category (men) that would monopolize the game without a
  quota, and a single protected category the quota exists to admit. Women and
  non-binary players are the *same* protected bucket, not two — no league's
  rules distinguish them, and none considers a third category. So the engine
  needs exactly two buckets: the capped/dominant one (`menCeiling`) and the
  floored/protected one (`womenFloor`). What the protected bucket is *called*
  ("women," "females," "women/non-binary," "non-male") and how it's displayed
  is a copy/vocabulary question to settle later — not a structural concern.
  The one place this assumption is under test is Volo's "1 woman" vs "1
  woman/non-binary" wording: if a non-binary player alone did *not* satisfy the
  floor, Volo would be the first three-category system. We expect it counts.
- **Voluntary short-siding is out of scope.** The app forecasts whether a
  turnout can field a legal team; nobody benches available players.

### Open items blocking the model

- **NY Coed** — play-down or forfeit when short on women? (sets `floorType`) +

- **Urban** — ~~exact play-down amount + keeper scoping/gender.~~ **Resolved:**

- **Volo** — does a non-binary player alone satisfy the floor? (the test of the
  two-category assumption — see Cross-cutting decisions) + keeper-inclusion
  confirm.
- **NYC Footy std** — keeper scoping (does a keeper count toward the 2 women?).
- **FLIP/50-50** — deferred, but the combined NYC Footy email asks the ratio
  keeper scoping, the FLIP 5-vs-4 number, play-down-vs-forfeit, and whether the
  min-players rule is inherited.

### Future milestone idea — league rules database + picker

(Richard's idea, 2026-07-22. Not scheduled; captured for later.)

Store the per-league rules as **data rather than code**, so adding a league is a
data-entry task, not a code change. This is the natural payoff of the canonical
engine above: each stored rule set is just one instance of the six parameters.

**Data shape.** city → league → rule set. A "rule set" is normally a side size
(5v5 / 7v7 / 9v9) but can also be a variant (50/50, FLIP, indoor, or any other
play with different rules). Each rule-set row holds the six engine parameters
for that format.

**UI.**
- A 2-option radio at the top: **"Choose league"** vs **"Enter rules manually."**
- "Choose league" → three hierarchical dropdowns: city → league → rule set.
- "Enter rules manually" → the config form exposing the six knobs directly (the
  escape hatch we already wanted for any unmodeled league).
- A small note + contact form: "Don't see your league? Email us the rules URL
  and we may add it." (Same "contact us" disclaimer we're deferring FLIP/50-50
  behind — so the picker absorbs it.)

**Maintenance.** Richard curates the DB. Adding a league = scrape its rules
across all side sizes and insert rows (Claude can generate the SQL / inserts).
One caveat from this exercise: scraping reliability varies — some league sites
are JavaScript apps a plain fetch can't read (Volo), so some entries will need a
manual paste rather than an automated scrape.

**Why it fits.** This unifies three things we reached separately: the
data-driven six-parameter engine (rows in the DB), the manual-entry escape
hatch, and the "contact us" disclaimer — all three become one picker with a
fallback.

**Risk that could kill this milestone (decide at the grill-me, not now).** Two
obstacles to populating the DB, and the second is the worse one:

1. *Bundled-JS / SPA sites* (Volo) that a plain fetch can't read. A one-time
   technical hurdle per site; LLMs make this far more tractable than it used to
   be, even if not fully automatable.
2. *Incomplete published rules* — the reason we're sending emails at all. This
   is human-in-the-loop and depends on officials replying, so it doesn't scale
   like scraping. Every new league risks a round of back-and-forth before its
   rows are trustworthy.

If curation proves too cumbersome, that's the case against building this at all.
There's a hedge, but it's narrower than it first looks, and the distinction is
load-bearing:

- *Missing whole leagues is fine.* The DB can cover a subset; any league not in
  it falls back to manual entry, so incomplete **coverage** degrades reach, not
  correctness.
- *Missing parameters within a row is not fine.* A ruleset row must be fully
  specified before it ships. You cannot store a half-filled row and prompt the
  captain to supply the gap: the parameter is missing precisely because it's an
  edge case the website doesn't cover, so the captain — who would go check that
  same website — can't answer it any better than we could. Worse, the knobs
  that go missing are almost always the "what happens when short on women" ones,
  the rare situations a captain may never have personally hit, so experience
  doesn't rescue them either. No copy, however good, fixes a question whose
  answer isn't knowable from the captain's sources.

Consequences: a DB entry is **all-or-nothing per row**, so the email round-trip
is a *prerequisite* for every league added, not optional polish — which raises
the per-league cost and is the real weight behind the kill risk. And manual
entry stays an operator tool for a fully-known league, never a per-field "we're
missing this, you tell us" prompt to captains.

The grill-me question, refined: not "can we make it complete?" but "is curating
a set of fully-pinned-down leagues worth it — given each one needs an email
round-trip first — versus just leaning on manual entry?" Decide then, not now.

### Related idea — a per-league provenance record (also for the grill-me)

Keep a permanent, source-controlled record per league holding:

1. URL(s) of the rules pages
2. The supporting text snippets copied from those pages
3. The clarifying emails we sent
4. The officials' responses
5. The parameter set we settled on

This is really "formalize what this file already is." Items 1, 2, 3, and 5 are
already here per league; only 4 (the replies) is missing, and it's pending. The
proposal promotes that from a working scratch file into a durable, structured
per-league record (one file per league, or a small schema) — which earns its
keep on its own as the audit trail for *why* each parameter was set, useful the
day a captain disputes a rule or a league quietly changes theirs, regardless of
any inference payoff.

**Speculative payoff.** With a large enough corpus of leagues that have all five
filled in, an LLM might start proposing #5 for a new league from #1 alone. Two
honest caveats:

- It can't *extract* a missing edge-case answer from a website that never
  stated it — same wall as before. What a corpus adds is **priors** ("most coed
  7v7 leagues play down one per missing woman"), so it can *predict* a likely
  default even when the specific site is silent. A guess from neighbours, not a
  fact from the source.
- So inference yields a **draft, not a shippable row.** Per the all-or-nothing
  rule above, a row still needs confirmation before it ships — so the payoff
  isn't skipping the email, it's better starting guesses and shorter/fewer
  questions. Probably not reliable enough to lean on, but worth weighing.

**The reliable win is the email, not the parameters.** Even when the inferred #5
can't be trusted enough to show a captain, the same pipeline *can* be trusted to
draft the clarifying email — exactly what happened this session. Extracting
snippets (#2), spotting the gaps, and writing a tight, answerable question (#3)
are things the LLM does well; only the final parameter set needs human
confirmation. Better still, the inferred guess can ride *inside* the email as a
proposed answer — "we read your rule as X; is that right?" — which is easier for
an official to reply to (yes / no / correction) than an open question, so it
lifts reply rates and makes replies faster to process. That compounds: better
priors → better confirm-style emails → more replies → more corpus. And it bears
